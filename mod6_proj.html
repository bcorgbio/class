Module 6 Project

Introduction

The ability to maintain a consistent and elevated body temperature (\(T_b\)) was an important evolutionary step in birds and mammals, permitting these taxa the ability to achieve higher metabolic output, regardless of ambient temperatures. Despite relatively consistent body temperatures, nearly all mammals engage in some form of heterothermy, etheir of the regional type (varying accross the body) or the temporal type (varying across time) (Aschoff 1982). Temporal heterothermy is often used as a strategy to modulate metabolic demands and reduce evaporative water loss (Ostrowski, Williams, and Ismael 2003). Over seasonal time scales, metabolic rate, and thus temperature, can be reduced when food availability declines in a form of torpor known as hibernation (McNab 1983). Reducing the metabolic rate reduces energy requirements and allows a hibernating endotherm to wait out the food-scarce (and often cold) seasons (Angilletta Jr and Angilletta 2009).


Even humans are temperal heterotherms as seen from the variability of \(T_b\) over diel cycles (panel C). From Hibi et al. (2017).



On diel time scales, fluxuations in \(T_b\) for mammals are nearly universal (Angilletta Jr and Angilletta 2009). These high \(T_b\) oscillations have been characterized by physiologists as a mechanism to reduce evaporative water loss, although other explainations have been tendered. By capturing and storing heat during the day and loosing heat at night through convenction, \(T_b\) oscillations reduce the need to cool during the day through evaporative processeses (Ostrowski, Williams, and Ismael 2003). An important prediction is born out of this functional hypothesis: larger mammals, with their higher capacitance (the ability to story energy), should have lower amplitudes associated with thes oscillations. Put simply, larger mammals need to discharge a smaller share of their stored heat energy to achieve this water-loss savings. Thus, we should expect some type of scaling of relationship between the difference between max daily \(T_b\) (\(T_{max}\)) and mininum daily \(T_b\) (\(T_{min}\), or \(T_{\Delta}\)) and mass (\(W\)).

Many inter-specific scaling relationships playout within species as well. The variation of mass-specific metabolic rate (\(M_b\) or \(\dot V_{O_2}\)) with \(W\) is one such scaling relationship. Larger species have lower mass-specific \(\dot V_{O_2}\) values than smaller species AND larger individuals within a particular species have lower mass-specific \(\dot V_{O_2}\) than small individuals (Aschoff 1982; Mortola and Lanthier 2004). The goals of this project follow this same inference: evaluate whether humans demonstrate a scaling relationship with respect to \(T_{\Delta}\) and \(W\) and whether mean \(T_{\Delta}\) values for humans and other mammals scale according to mass.

Methods

To capture \(T_b\) data, you must design and construct a portable Arduino-based temperature data aqusition system (DAQ). Your device should record body axial temperature data over the course of 1 hour for each of your team members.

Sampling times should correspond to predictably high \(T_b\) (i.e., 30 minutes when the subject is active, say in the middle of the day) and predictably low \(T_b\) (i.e., 30 minutes early in the morning, say 4 AM, before you typically get up—sorry! Set those alarms). This won’t capture the full diurnal cycle, but will reduce the DAQ’s running time while recording the important points.

DAQ Design Components

  1. DS18B20 temperature sensor
  2. Arduion Uno microcontroller
  3. SD storage module
  4. 9V battery power cable

Assembly for this temperature logger is almost entirely captured in this tutorialthis tutorial. To this prototype, we must add battery power. That’s easy enough: merely connect the battery power cable in the battery barrel connector on the Arduino (see pic below).

Data Collection and Analysis

Intra-specific Scaling

To evaluate the scaling relationship between \(T_{\Delta}\) and mass, construct a various models that describe the relationship between mass (\(W\)) and the differnce between mean \(T_{high}\) and \(T_{low}\) for all participants in the study (all the students in the class). Yes, we’ll be relying on the rest of your class for these data. After completing data collection, each group should submit their data as .csv text files to this google directory by Tuesday, October 20th.

Rememember from the Module 5 project description, that saving data files with a predictable format containing metadata is essential to a clean and easy workflow in R. The metadata needed for this project include your team name, subject, and mass. Therefore, please contruct the file names thusly: "TeamName_Subject#_Time_Mass.csv".

  • TeamName: your team name, the same for each file your team submits.
  • Subject#: a subject number that uniquely identifies the participant anonymously (e.g., “Subject1”)
  • Time: Time of day, i.e., “day” or “night”.
  • Mass: The subject’s mass in kilograms (an estimate is fine).
  • Make sure each of the metadata are separated by and underscore, "_".

Once all files of these data can be downloaded and from this directory, analysis can commence. (- 5 pts for not submitting on time).

Inter-specific scaling

From the class-wide values, compute the mean mass and mean \(T_{\Delta}\) for our human subjects and add these values to a dataset that includes the same data from 51 species of non-human mammals from (Mortola and Lanthier 2004).
